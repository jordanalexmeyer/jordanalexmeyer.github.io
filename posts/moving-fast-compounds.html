A thesis on execution velocity

Moving fast compounds.

Subject
Why small speed advantages become exponential leads
Model
Five-year simulation from seed to Series C
Result
A 20% edge becomes a 4.5x output gap

Sam Altman once tweeted that moving fast compounds. It's one of those claims that sounds true and gets retweeted, but is rarely proven. The math, it turns out, works — and the mechanism is more interesting than the slogan suggests.

The easy version goes like this. If two companies grow exponentially but at different rates, the absolute gap between them grows exponentially. The ratio of their sizes also grows exponentially. If you ship at rate k and your competitor ships at k/s, the ratio of your size to theirs is:

R_you(t) / R_them(t) = e^(k(1 − 1/s) t)

This is true and useful as a first cut. But it's abstract. It assumes growth just happens at some rate, which isn't how startups actually work. Startups grow by hiring. They raise a round, hire harder, produce more, hit the next milestone, raise again. The interesting question is: what does a speed advantage look like inside that real structure?

A small per-person edge becomes a bigger round, which becomes a bigger team, which becomes a faster next round. — The compounding loop

A better model

Imagine two companies, both founded with five engineers. Each engineer produces "units of work" per day — abstract, but you can read it as shipped features, qualified leads, customer conversations, code, whatever your company's output actually is. Headcount grows continuously at a rate that steps up after each raise, because bigger rounds fund bigger hiring plans. Funding rounds aren't free events — they're unlocked when cumulative work crosses thresholds that represent proof of traction.

The load-bearing assumption here is that work correlates strongly with revenue. Ship more, sell more, grow more. That's what a VC is actually underwriting at the next round — not raw output, but the revenue trajectory the output produces. When the model says "cumulative work crosses a threshold," read it as "the company hit the ARR milestone that unlocks the round." Everywhere downstream — larger rounds, higher valuations, faster hiring — flows from that same link.

The thresholds are calibrated against Carta's 2024 fundraising data. The median startup now takes about 18 months from seed to Series A, and two more years to Series B. Top-quartile companies can raise Series A in under a year, and hyperscalers (think Stripe, OpenAI, Anthropic) compress the first three rounds into about three years. That's the spread this model captures.

The fast company's engineers each produce 1.0 units per day. The slow company's each produce 0.8 — a 20% edge per head. That's a modest difference. You wouldn't notice it in a weekly standup. In a quarter, you might barely measure it.

But here's where it gets interesting. Because the fast company grows faster, it raises larger rounds at higher valuations. That's not a modeling assumption — it's how venture capital actually works. Growth rate drives valuation, and valuation drives round size. The fast company can hire at 12%/month post-Series A; the slow company, funded at a lower valuation, can only manage 5%/month. After Series B, the fast company's higher capitalization lets it attract better talent and invest in better tooling, so per-person productivity bumps another 15%.

Both companies hit their milestones the same way: by actually crossing a cumulative work threshold. The slow company eventually reaches every milestone the fast company does — it just takes longer.

Why the gap widens

The chart shows three compounding effects stacked on top of each other. Each is modest on its own. Together they produce a gap that a 20% per-person edge, considered in isolation, would never predict.

01

Earlier milestones

The fast company hits Series A four months sooner. By Series B the lead is a full year. By Series C, a year and a half. Small edges, applied to a threshold, compound into widening time-gaps at each round.

02

Faster hiring

Higher growth rates earn higher valuations, which fund faster hiring. The fast company can grow headcount at 12% a month post-Series A; the slow company, at a lower valuation, has to stay at 5%. The same delta shows up at every subsequent stage.

03

Better talent

Fast-growing companies with strong capitalization attract higher-quality hires and invest in better tooling. Per-person output rises another 15% after Series B, multiplying everything downstream.

The slogan, properly stated

"Move fast" is usually framed as motivation — something founders tell each other to justify all-nighters. But motivation is the wrong frame. Speed isn't a personality trait or a cultural value. It's a position. Your speed determines which equilibrium your company ends up in.

The fast company in this model doesn't end the simulation slightly ahead. It ends with 4.5x the output, a team 2.6x the size, at Series C eighteen months earlier, with compounding tailwinds that will continue to widen the gap next year and the year after. The slow company eventually reaches every milestone — it just reaches them later, at a smaller scale, with less momentum going into whatever's next. Not because it's doing anything wrong, but because it started 20% slower per head, and the funding ladder rewards the fast company with every rung.

Speed doesn't just get you to the same place faster. It gets you to a bigger place, because scale is a function of time. — The claim, in one sentence

This is why founders who obsess over shaving a week off a launch, or closing a round a month early, or shipping one more feature per quarter, aren't being neurotic. They're responding correctly to the structure of the game they're playing. A small edge per day, sustained for a few years, puts you in a different league.

Moving fast compounds. The math works.
