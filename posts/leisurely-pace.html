A biography note · On time

Get it done by tomorrow morning.

Subject
Chung Ju-yung, founder of Hyundai
Source
His autobiography, one Monday morning
The claim
The whole philosophy in six words

Every Monday at our team kickoff, I share a quote from a book I've been reading. This week's came from the autobiography of the man who built Hyundai.

Who was Chung Ju-yung?

If you don't know the name, think of him as the Korean Elon Musk — except he built his empire out of a country that had been flattened by war. Chung founded Hyundai and became one of the most consequential Koreans of the last century. He built much of modern Korea's physical infrastructure: roads, power plants, shipyards.

He lived through the Great Depression, the Japanese occupation, the Korean War, and a sequence of regime changes that stripped him of his businesses more than once.

He kept going anyway.

The quote

Here is what he wrote about how most people spend their time:

Everybody wants to enjoy life comfortably at a leisurely pace. However, I think there is nothing more foolish than living a life according to the mantra of "doing enough just to get by," not knowing how precious one's time is.

These remarkable people did not take two or three lifetimes to accomplish what they did. They did it in one.

When work needs to be done, there are those who get it done immediately and others who procrastinate… For these reasons, I never give my workers a lot of time to finish a task. "Get it done by tomorrow morning."

Read that last line again. It is the whole philosophy in six words.

Procrastination compounds

Procrastination is almost invisible in the short term. A day slips. Then a week. You barely notice. Compounded over years, it becomes the quiet difference between companies that ship and companies that don't — between the person who could have come in first and the one who finishes last.

Chung's insight wasn't that talent is rare. It was that time is the one thing everyone has in equal measure, and almost nobody uses it seriously.

These remarkable people did not take two or three lifetimes to accomplish what they did. They did it in one. — Chung Ju-yung

How we try to live this at Extend

At my company, we try to apply this by setting tight deadlines and holding each other to them. We don't always get it right. That's exactly why we keep reminding each other. I'm still working on it myself.

The hardest part isn't believing the quote. It's acting like I believe it.

A question for your Monday

What is the thing you have been letting slip at a "leisurely pace"?

The honest answer is probably also the most important thing you could work on this week.

Get it done by tomorrow morning.
